The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the distribution of sample means approximates a normal distribution as the sample size becomes larger, regardless of the shape of the population distribution.​

It establishes that for independent and indentically disributed random variables, the sampling distribution of the standarized sample mean tends towards the standard normal distribution, even if the original variables themselves are not normally distributed.

In other words, suppose that a large sample of observations is obtained, each observation being randomly produced in a way that does not depend on the values of the other observations, and that the average (airthmetic mean) of the observed values is computed. If this procedure is performed many times, resulting in a collection of observed averages, the central limit theorem says that if the sample size was large enough, the probability distribution of these averages will closely approximate a normal distribution.

Let’s use a Python simulation to demonstrate the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) with randomly generated patient data. For the sake of simplicity, we’ll assume the distribution of patient stays follows a non-normal distribution (let’s say, a gamma distribution, which can model waiting times).

First, we’ll generate the population data:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Set a seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# Parameters for the gamma distribution 
shape, scale = 2, 2.  

# Generate the "population"
population = np.random.gamma(shape, scale, 1000000)

plt.plot(population)

[<matplotlib.lines.Line2D at 0x7fd19e11dd80>]

Now, we’ll repeatedly sample from this population and record the sample means:

# Number of samples
n_samples = 1000

# Size of each sample
sample_size = 5

# Store the sample means here
sample_means = []

for _ in range(n_samples):
    # Draw a random sample from the population
    sample = np.random.choice(population, sample_size)
    
    # Compute the sample mean
    sample_means.append(np.mean(sample))

np.mean(sample_means)

4.238665738079807

Finally, we can visualize the distribution of sample means to see if it approximates a normal distribution:

According to the Central Limit Theorem, the histogram should show a distribution that looks approximately normal, centered around the true population mean, which we can compute with np.mean(population). This demonstrates that even though the lengths of stay were originally gamma-distributed, the means of samples from this population are normally distributed.

Keep in mind that in real-life scenarios, you wouldn’t have access to the whole “population” as we do in this simulation. Instead, you’d take a sample from the population, calculate the sample mean and use that to estimate the population mean, as described in the Central Limit Theorem.

plt.hist(sample_means, bins=30, density=True)
plt.xlim(0, 10)
plt.xlabel('Sample Mean Length of Stay')
plt.ylabel('Frequency')
plt.title('Distribution of Sample Means of Patient Length of Stay')
plt.show()
print('Population mean is')
print(np.mean(population))
